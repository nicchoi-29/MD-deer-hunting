Though hunters have been villainized in both My Cousin Vinny and Bambi, they're important allies of Maryland's Department of Natural Resources, which is responsible for managing the state’s deer population. Concerns over deer range from backyard garden nuisances to larger agricultural disruptions to increased risk of vehicle crashes to the spread of zoonotic tickborne diseases, including but not limited to Lyme.

Relatively high numbers of reported cases each year have placed Maryland in one of the top 10 states for Lyme disease, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

Incidence rates of Lyme disease in Maryland have remained above the national average from 2015 to 2022, based on the most recent data provided by the Johns Hopkins Lyme and Tickborne Diseases Dashboard, with over 2400 new cases reported in 2023, according to the CDC.

And these are just the reported cases. The prognostic bullseye rash associated with Lyme doesn’t appear on everyone. “As many as 40% of Lyme disease cases are not diagnosed until a later stage,” at which point symptoms can be difficult to trace to the disease, according to the Lyme Wellness Initiative.

Something about deer

In Maryland, as throughout the rest of the United States, the white-tailed deer is the most common deer species. These deer have been making their way more frequently into suburbia, increasing concern about their role as hosts to disease-carrying ticks.

A tickborne disease is the category name for an infectious disease “caused by pathogens transmitted to humans and animals through the bite of infected ticks.” A tick that carries a tickborne pathogen doesn’t mean it was born infected. It means it fed on infected mice and other small mammals, and can now transmit the pathogen by biting a host.

Deer sometimes serve as reservoirs for tickborne diseases, like anaplasmosis, and can transmit the pathogen to the ticks feeding on them. When it comes to other tickborne pathogens, deer can become infected without showing disease. And sometimes, depending on the pathogen, deer may not become infected at all. In fact, a 2023 study found that the blood of white-tailed deer could kill strains of the bacterium that causes Lyme.

The way deer facilitate the spread of tickborne diseases is by transporting infected ticks geographically, which can put those ticks in contact with more humans.

A 2017 found that exposure to ticks in the suburbs was greatest around the home. A 2022 study found that deer in the suburbs actually live and sleep in those neighborhoods. Other studies have found that deer seem to be attracted to edge habitats in the suburbs. An edge habitat, or ecotonal area, is where two ecosystems, like a backyard and residential woodland meet. These habitats provide deer with food and protection from predators.

“Numerous wildlife species no longer just tolerate human presence but in fact seek out suburban development due to a greater abundance of habitat diversity,” write Megan Linske and Scott Williams in their 2024 study.

White-tailed deer also have a "high fecundity rate," meaning they can and often do produce many offspring. Managing their numbers has become a priority for many states.

Maryland-Specific Context

In response to deer moving into more areas populated by humans, Maryland’s Department of Natural Resources wrote in its 2020-2034 “White-Tailed Deer Management Plan” that relative to “non-lethal management techniques (such as contraceptives and sterilization) and non-hunting mortality (disease, injuries and predators) . . . [l]ethal control of deer via regulated hunting remains the most effective way to balance the deer population with environmental and cultural concerns” at scale.

The state is divided into two hunting regions: Region A and Region B. The split occurs in Washington County along a border formed by three major connected roads. The land east of that border is regulated under Region A and the land that sits west under Region B.

In 2020, the state re-established the Suburban Deer Management Zone, which sits within Region B. With the state subdivided into 23 counties, this zone comprises the state’s five most populated counties: Montgomery, Howard, Baltimore, Prince George’s, and Anne Arundel. In this zone, the bag limit for antlerless deer is unlimited for archery hunters during hunting season.

Antlered vs. Antlerless

When it comes to hunting white-tailed deer, the state uses an antlered classification to identify a deer as a buck, or an adult male.

On the other hand, the antlerless classification can serve as a partial proxy for adult female deer, or does. However, button bucks (young male deer named after the “buttons” on their heads where their antlers will eventually grow) and female fawns can also fall into this category.

Though it was once (and perhaps is still) taboo to hunt a doe, harvest numbers in Maryland for anterless deer have been historically higher than those for antlered deer, at least in the Region B counties.

The priority in harvesting doe was stated in the Department of Natural Resources's "White-Tailed Deer Management Plan”:

“Along with providing ample opportunities to harvest deer, the department continued focusing harvest on antlerless deer. Seasons and bag limits remained structured to prioritize female harvest, and outreach materials, including press releases and web information, stressed the importance of antlerless harvest to help maintain deer numbers.”

While bag limits can vary each hunting season, they are largely more limited when it comes to antlerless deer in Region A, which comprises the western part of the state. For instance, for the upcoming 2026-2027 hunting season, hunters in Region A may bag only up to two antlerless deer and only one antlerless deer per day. In Region B, however, archery hunters may bag 15 during the season, as well as an unlimited number of anterless deer in the Suburban Deer Management Zone. Hunters using other weapons, such as muzzleloaders and firearms, may each harvest 10 this season.
