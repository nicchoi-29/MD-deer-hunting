Managing Maryland's Deer Population

Hunting serves as the primary method for controling the state's white-tailed deer population

Though hunters have been villainized in both My Cousin Vinny and Bambi (and understandably so, for there’s a reason why the expression "doe-eyed" exists), they play an important role in managing Maryland’s deer population. Concerns over deer range from backyard garden nuisances to larger agricultural disruptions to increased risk of vehicle crashes to the spread of zoonotic tickborne diseases, including but not limited to Lyme.

Relatively high numbers of reported cases each year have placed Maryland in one of the top 10 states for Lyme disease, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

Incidence rates of Lyme disease in Maryland have remained above the national average from 2015 to 2022, based on the most recent data provided through the Johns Hopkins Lyme and Tickborne Diseases Dashboard, with over 2400 new cases reported in 2023, according to the CDC.

And these are just the reported cases. The prognostic bullseye rash associated with Lyme doesn’t appear on everyone. “As many as 40% of Lyme disease cases are not diagnosed until a later stage,” at which point symptoms can be difficult to trace to the disease, according to the Lyme Wellness Initiative.

Something about deer

In Maryland, as throughout the rest of the United States, the white-tailed deer is the most common deer species. These deer have been making their way more frequently into suburbia, increasing concern about their role as host to disease-carrying ticks.

A tickborne disease is the category name for an infectious disease “caused by pathogens transmitted to humans and animals through the bite of infected ticks.” A tick that carries a tickborne pathogen doesn’t mean it was born infected. It means it fed on infected mice and other small mammals, and can now transmit the pathogen by biting a host.

Deer sometimes serve as reservoirs for tickborne diseases, like anaplasmosis, and can transmit the pathogens to the ticks feeding on them. When it comes to other tickborne pathogens, deer can become infected without showing disease. And sometimes, depending on the pathogen, deer may not become infected at all. In fact, a 2023 study found that the blood of white-tailed deer could kill strains of the bacterium that causes Lyme.

The way deer facilitate the spread of tickborne diseases is by transporting infected ticks geographically, which can then put those ticks in contact with humans.

A 2017 found that exposure to ticks in the suburbs was greatest around the home. A 2022 study found that deer in the suburbs actually live and sleep in those neighborhoods. Other studies have found that deer seem to be attracted to edge habitats in the suburbs. An edge habitat, or ecotonal area, is where two ecosystems, like a backyard and residential woodland meet. These habitats provide deer with food and protection from predators.

“Numerous wildlife species no longer just tolerate human presence but in fact seek out suburban development due to a greater abundance of habitat diversity,” write Megan Linske and Scott Williams in their 2024 study.

White-tailed deer also have a "high fecundity rate," meaning they can and often do produce many offspring. Managing their numbers has become a priority for many states.
